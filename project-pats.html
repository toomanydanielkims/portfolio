Project Overview

With the Visa Creative Team, an accessible VR experience for Patriots fans utilizing Google Cardboard and Youtube’s 360video features. This was an incredible opportunity to create design a cross-platform experience, from mobile to desktop to VR.

Problem

The brief

As a service brand that prides itself in being apart of everyday life, Visa wanted to showcase how their newest Bank of America Card brings people closer to the things they love. In conjunction with their global brand message of “everywhere you want to be” Visa wanted to virtually bring their users closer to where they wanted to be with utilizing the Youtube Apps’s 360 VR. Our team at BBDO was tasked with creating the mobile and desktop landing pages for the experience.

Lots of fans, lots of devices

We needed to ensure fans could jump into the experience with whatever device they had, so we spent significant time planning the onboarding process.

Tailored branding

We made separate sites for Visa and for Bank of America with Visa. We needed to ensure assets were versatile for each brand message.

Spreading the word

The combination of intriguing new technology with a famous fanbase generated instant clicks.

As an art director my specific roles were in designing the mobile and desktop onboarding process and creating the VR title and instruction screens. I worked closely with our interaction designer to wireframe, prototype and iterate on designs until the VR experience was launched via press. With Visa’s global reach in mind and incredibly diverse user base, we put accessibility at the front of all of our design decisions. As we designed our main goals were:

Consistency across a myriad of devices

As any football or Patriots fan could become a potential user, we needed to try to accommodate as many platforms as possible so nobody would be left out. Rather than individual apps a responsive landing page would be needed to onboard users.

Streamlined for the fans

We needed to find the right balance between promoting our brand sponsor and getting users where they wanted to be as easily and with as little pain points as possible.

Don't break the immersion

With all user experiences immersion is important, but especially with the hype of immersion in VR, we needed to ensure the onboarding experience (before their headsets wiped away to a new world) also feels immersive in its 2D state.

Empathize

Asking the fans

A key question we started with was how to address the different comfort levels with new tech people have. We wanted to discover potential painpoints users will come across during onboarding. We had a feeling that simple intuitive instructions for some users could become moments of frustration for others with less familiarity with new technology. The average NFL TV Viewer is 47 years old. While the love for football may be equally as passionate across all age groups, we suspected that their ability to navigate new technology might not be as equal so we knew looking into age demographics would be crucial.

For the fans

Our findings from research and interviews led us to two target demographics: tech-savy casual fans who could easily jump in to the experience and die-hard fans who are less comfortable with emerging tech but eager to see their favorite team in a new way.

Takeaways from Research

Takeaways

Browser Support

For desktops, 360 videos were best experienced on youtube.com via google chrome. 360Videos will work on recent versions of Chrome, Opera, Firefox, and Internet Explorer, but Safari was notably not supported at the time.

Android Dominance

Youtube’s 360 Videos were accessible in mobile on both iOS and Android via the Youtube app. 80% of smartphones ran Android. With an iPhone or Android smartphone, when you access a Youtube link, it automatically opens the Youtube app which is usually preinstalled on most phones and tablets. While clicking links and opening YouTube were simple enough, the likely painpoints would come from confusion about which devices supported which functions.

Individualized Onboarding

Since the Google Cardboard experience would be limited to Android devices, we would need to ensure we created individualized onboarding experiences for iPhone and Android users.

Information Architecture

Getting there quick

We would need to ensure users were taken to the proper landing pages depending on their device. Detailed instructions and system requirements would need to be provided per device.

Onboarding

Considering the user demographics inexperience with the new technology, having more instructions would always be a better scenario than not having enough.

If users were not onboarded correctly their experience could range from to inconvenient to a dizzying headache.

Hands on with Google Cardboard

I was able to test out google cardboard for myself. I was personally surprised by how accessible the technology was, hence cardboard. The iconic google cardboard shape was something that’s instantly recognizable and made me decide to use that specific icon when possible.
Iterations

Where to draw the line

In the first iteration I became fixated on the idea of creating immersion and tried out a few techniques to blend the instructions into the field. I’m glad I could explore these options in the beginning, as they helped me later prioritize functionality and legibility over artistic touches.

Based on the IA, we made simple wireframes

Helpful instructions

With unfamiliar technology, you can only slim down the instructions so much. To make the necessary instructions and disclaimers less daunting we moved away from generic lists and used verbs to lay out each step.

Implementing immersion

We chose wide fish-eye football field background to complement the immerse footage that the user would soon be able to interact with.

Learnings

Going forward

After working for a long time in traditional media, this exciting experience has opened the door to a new way of user-centered design thinking. With the endless list of devices users use to access content on the internet, creating responsive designs and friendly user journeys can be a daunting task. But it’s also very rewarding as you tackle each pain point to create a cohesive experience.

When working with new technology, clear and accessible documentation helps. Since the release of google cardboard, VR has come a long way and people are becoming increasingly familiar with the new technology. There will always be new and exciting technologies around the corner and there will always be accessible and friendly ways to introduce them.
