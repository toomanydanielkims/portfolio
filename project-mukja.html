Social distancing has led to a renewed interest in home cooking, but for many young people cooking is a daunting task. From sifting through thousands of recipes online to hunting down the right ingredients amidst stay at home orders, I wanted to find a way to make cooking easier for the those with less experience in the kitchen. I focused on creating a solution in the form of an app. Stay at Home Chef is an app that focuses on making cooking more accessible and simple by finding recipes tailored to each user and presenting them in a playful yet intuitive manner. It suggests recipes based on ingredients you already have in your kitchen.

Making the kitchen less daunting

I focused on alleviating this primary obstacle as well as a few others that would make a users kitchen experience more enjoyable.

Takeaways from Research

Finding the chef in all of us

After clustering the information from interviews and secondary research sources, the connections I drew led me to these key insights.

Too many options isn’t always a good thing

Simplifying the recipe search process and limiting how many recipe results show up would help fight user fatigue.

Cooking is a process of personal growth

The brand and tone presented to the user should be encouraging and remind the user that cooking is a journey. Supportive language and playful imagery would help to achieve this.

It comes down to the ingredients

Users seem to feel most held back on cooking because they don’t have the ingredients on hand. Not having ingredents ends the cooking journey for most people.

User Testing Feedback

Swipe if you're hungry

A consistent frustration from users in the early stages of testing was not being able to go back to a recipe they swiped over. To fix this painpoint I changed the swipes to like and dislike swipes largely popularized by the dating app Tinder. This allowed users to swipe right and like as many recipes as they wanted and compare their favorites at the end, or save some for the next time they’re hungry.

Playful gestures

Swiping left removes the recipe, swiping right saves it to be viewed later. Liked recipes can be selected or saved for later.

Development

Developing for iOS

Where possible I utilized Apple’s already defined structures to ensure the user’s gestures and inputs felt as natural as possible. I referenced Apple’s Human Guidelines that users are familiar with even if unconsciously, such as toggles, large titles and the easily accessible scrollable lists.

Finding a fitting recipe database

An obstacle I faced as a solo product developer was finding a recipe database I could use without consistent funding as most work on a subscription service. The API I initially utilized was from edamam, but without a paid subscription I users were limited to a certain amount of searches per day (API fetch requests capped). I ended up using the open-source mealDB to avoid any search caps, but upon official launch I plan to upgrade or move to another database.

Keeping animations light and snappy

I used AirBnb’s Lottie API to implement efficient and low file-size animated JSON files. Gifs and videos often weigh down apps that are encouraged to be light and snappy, but using JSON files through Lottie that rewrite the animations in code bring fluid and eye-catching animations to an app without the large memory sizes. With SwiftUI Lottie works with xCode’s Swift Package Manager, so installing through cocoaPods were not necessary (previously installing and loading cocoaPods would slow down the prototyping process).

Responsive layouts

Apple’s new SwiftUI introduces declarative syntax that allow for more responsive layout designs across all Apple devices. As users are becoming more and more accustomed to accessing apps from any of their devices, in the future I’d like to expand compatibility with Apple watch devices and macOS devices.

Positive feedback

The latest feedback received from users has been extremely positive, with many noting the that they will add the app to their cooking routine.

What's next?

To accomodate the users who are new to cooking, in the future I’d like to incorporate searching for recipes by Youtube videos. Many users noted that their current cooking journey starts on Youtube. An obstacle to this would be that cooking content creators don’t follow any established pattern that would lend itself to fetching for specific recipe data but I look forward to tackling this with research and testing until I land on a sound solution.

Where possible I utilized Apple’s already defined structures to ensure the user’s gestures and inputs felt as natural as possible. I referenced Apple’s Human Guidelines that iOS users are familiar with, even if only unconsciously, such as toggles, large titles and the easily accessible scrollable lists. This was my first time using Apple’s testflight service, but it won’t be my last as it is an incredible tool to connect with users remotely from their own devices. Nothing compares to being in a room together during user testing, but the reach that Apple’s testflight service has proved extremely valuable during this remote working period.
